Evolution and the Argument Against Psi

On The Lack of Evidence for the Evolution of Psi as

 an Argument Against the Reality of the Paranormal

Michael Levin

Abstract

     There seem to be three major lines of argument against the reality of the para-

normal (psi): the a priori improbability argument, the lack of evidence argument,

and the evolutionary argument. The evolutionary argument states that if the vari-

ous paranormal powers or faculties were real ways of acquiring information and

affecting the outside world, such abilities would have been vastly improved by

evolution and would be in widespread use by the biosphere today. The argument

proceeds to note that given the very brittle, confusing, and sporadic nature of psy-

chic phenomena that have been reported, as compared to the robust and immedi-

ately useful ways that biosystems manipulate other energetic modalities (such as

sound, light, etc.), it is improbable that psi represents a real feature of the world.

This is a serious argument, and it requires a response. In this paper I discuss sev-

eral possible replies to such an objection. I take the most likely explanation to be

that psi is a feature of the mind, not of the brain. Thus, it is only indirectly af-

fected by biological phenotype, and thus potentially uncoupled from improvement

by biophysical evolution.

jor lines are evident: the a priori improbability argument, the lack of evidence ar-

gument, and the lack of evolution of psi argument. Although none of these argu-

A priori Probability Argument

     The first argument against Psi is often associated with scientists (or laypeople)

who are very impressed with the current state and direction of science. The argu-

ment itself can be condensed as follows: science has been very successful at pre-

dicting and controlling the external world. It has done this using certain basic

metaphysical principles (such as insisting on third person objective descriptions of

processes, repeatability, restricted causality, etc.). Thus, when a parapsychological

experiment is described where some of these principles are violated, the most rea-

sonable course (according to this argument) is to reject it, no matter how tight the

experimental design was, or how much we can trust in the honesty of the experi-

menters. In this line of thought, the a priori probability of some phenomenon that

contradicts the basic physicalist principles is so low that fraud or some unknown

experimental error must have been present. Even if no such problem is found,

some conclude that there must have been, because the likelihood of the existence

of psi is so low.

     There is not room here to go into all of the problems with this line of reason-

ing, but several are evident immediately. First, this argument stands no matter

what kind of experiment is presented. Thus, it is unfalsifiable and therefore should

be very suspect as a scientific explanation. Second, this argument effectively

serves as a dead-end to discovery of additional successful metaphysical princi-

ples. That is, if we reject any new principles on the basis that they are not in-

The Lack of Evidence Argument

     The second argument states that although psi is possible in principle, the evi-

dence for it is insufficient, and thus in keeping with Occamâs razor, we omit psi

from our ontology pending further data. There isnât much new to be said about

this argument; to my mind, the evidence (e.g., see, Wolman, 1977, and references

therein) is quite sufficient to suggest that Psi is real and deserving of further

study. Palmer (1987) discusses the logical and philosophical flaws in this view as

well as its subtler variant, "extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence."

     The fact that the evidence for psi as well as theoretical frameworks for inter-

preting results are less than satisfactory is perhaps unsurprising: given the fact that

the human mind is finite and conditioned (by environment as well as biological

design) to think along certain lines, there have to be features of the world that do

not fit easily into our ways of making sense of the Universe. When we study the

Universe using any specific set of principles and strategies, the phenomena that

yield to explanation first are precisely those which are tractable given those strate-

gies (e.g., the current findings of physics). There will surely be some features of

the Universe that will not be easily dealt with using those approaches; these are

the phenomena that will be less well understood at any given point in time. Thus,

it is to be expected that at this time, for example, there are certain areas that are

well understood (basic physics), other areas which we do not really even know

The Lack of Evidence for the Evolution of Psi as an Argument

     The third argument against the reality of psi, the argument from evolution, is

rarely voiced, but I think it is much more cogent than the previous two. This line

of reasoning (original source unknown) asks us to consider the immense power of

evolution in optimizing the use of various sensory and motor modalities for inter-

acting with the outside world. Through differential reproduction combined with

natural selection on geological time scales (Dobzhansky, 1977; Laszlo, 1987), al-

most every conceivable way of acquiring information or manipulating the envi-

ronment has been invented and perfected by living beings (Preston-Mafham &

Preston-Mafham, 1993). In the discussion below I provide only single well-

known examples of creatures that utilize any given strategy; zoology and ethology

provide a myriad of further striking instances.

     Electromagnetic radiation, from infra-red (snakes) through visible light (hu-

man beings) to ultra-violet (bees), as well as magnetic fields (birds) and electric

fields (bees) are used to collect information about the outside world. Likewise,

material vibrations (whether through air, such as human speech; water, as in

whales; or solids, such as spiders that tap messages through plant stalks) and bio-

chemical messages (such as pheromones) are also exploited as a sensory modal-

ity. In attempting to manipulate and influence the outside world, living beings

make use of motion (whether self-propelled, air- or water-borne, or by hitching a

ride on other creatures), traps (spider webs), chemical warfare (insects), projec-

tiles (tongues, squirts of liquid), and so on.

     All of these ingenious adaptations make use of physical features of the real

world (such as the fact that electromagnetic radiation emitted from objects carries

information about the object, etc.). All of them require extremely complex sen-

The intricacy of these organs and systems, and the complexity which is required

are a constant source of amazement to biologists. Thus, it is seen that evolution is

likely to provide the eventual appearance of whatever biological organ or struc-

psi is a real ingredient in the world, why is it that creatures have not evolved the

ability to use it effectively and consistently?

     It would seem that such abilities as clairvoyance (both spatial and temporal)

and psychokinesis would be invaluable aids in locating food or mates and in

avoiding predators or subduing prey. The evolutionary pressure to evolve skills

such as knowing where predators are likely to be, or being able to move objects

from a safe distance, should be immense. In parallel with other physical abilities,

they should have evolved to the point where we would now observe their consis-

tent and effective use, rather than the sporadic, unpredictable, and mostly uncon-

trollable display of psi that occurs in most situations. Thus, the argument goes, we

can conclude that psi is not a real feature of the physical world. This is a powerful

argument that requires a reply, if parapsychological research is to be taken seri-

ously by the mainstream scientific community.

Replies to the Lack of Evidence for the Evolution of Psi

     Rather than focus on a defense of any particular reply to this objection, I dis-

cuss the pros and cons of several commonly suggested ways to resolve this di-

lemma, as they provide fertile ground for thinking about psi as it might be embed-

ded within the world biology and physics have opened for us. 

     The first possibility is to deny neo-Darwinian evolution as we understand it.

Although there are clearly some problems (both of a philosophical and empirical

     Another possible reply is to deny the implicit premise that we do not observe

psi in the everyday lives of the animal world. In fact, there are a variety of phe-

nomena known to biologists that have no current explanation. These include the

amazingly coordinated activity of ants in a colony (Holldobler & Wilson, 1990),

their seemingly paranormal ability to know the status of the queen (Marais, 1973),

the ability of ants and other small insects to traverse huge distances through ter-

rain (grass, rocks, etc., which completely obscures their vision) and to return

home, the ability of organisms to migrate over immense distances to places they

have never seen, the ability of life-mating lizards to find the same mate year after

year when returning from a migration, the too-rapid flight adjustment of birds at

the rear of a flock to the movements of a leader they cannot see, the apparent abil-

ity of some animals to predict their ownerâs behavior as well as major disasters

(discussed in Sheldrake, 1994), and others. Naturally, most working biologists as-

sume that these are all accomplished in some as yet unknown way by the kinds of

physical senses and organs with which we are familiar. But, it is possible that be-

havior and abilities we take for granted may in fact require and make use of "para-

normal" phenomena (for a review, see Morris, 1970). This reply is somewhat

unsatisfying because it fails to explain why we (human beings) do not enjoy reli-

able and conscious use of such faculties (as we do with other sensory and motor

modalities), and why such seemingly useful powers are not in constant and obvi-

ous use but are restricted to a small class of phenomena that do not yet have an

obvious physico-chemical explanation.

of psi as a channel for information transfer and influence exertion may be very

different from the ones used by the physical sensori-motor systems, thus affecting

their spread throughout the biological community. For example, all forms of en-

     Another potential problem arising from the uniqueness of psi as a sensory-

evolutionarily inefficient compared to more normal methods in use by living be-

and sporadic paranormal knowledge often seem to occur spontaneously and with-

seems plausible that the benefits associated with such powers may overshadow

their energy requirements, especially compared with all of the energy that must go

into an organismâs creating and then using organs for more mundane functions.

     One of the few authors who touch on the evolution of psi is Broughton (1993),

who discusses the view that ESP should be seen as a need-serving human ability,

useful in things like intuitive decision-making, seemingly fortuitous but helpful

many other, less subtle, ways for psi to be useful in life. Broughton also suggests

that the unconscious, limited, and subtly hidden nature of psi ability represents

some kind of evolutionarily stable strategy (ESS) -- a tradeoff given that others

have the ability as well, and that it may not be profitable for an animal to adver-

tise the fact that it has these powers. This is an interesting idea, but it should be

noted that competition among members of a population all of whom possess some

particular skill usually leads to an evolutionary "arms race," as opposed to a mu-

tual curtailing of its use. The dynamics leading to such arms races are so strong

that they frequently occur despite large disadvantages to the organisms. For exam-

     One other objection may be entertained by those who do not limit themselves

to the data of laboratory parapsychology but work within some theoretical frame-

work which includes psi, such as Theosophy (Besant, 1951), Anthroposophy

(Steiner, 1961, 1981), Yoga (Ramacharaka, 1907), and Rosicrucianism (Heindel,

1956). It may then be considered that psi, unlike electromagnetism, sound, etc., is

not value-neutral with respect to "moral" qualities. Perhaps the use of psi is some-

how restricted to those (human beings?) who have perfected themselves according

to some moral code; or, perhaps it can only be used for "good" actions (thus it

may not be possible to trap prey using PK). Although some dualists may be sym-

pathetic to such worldviews, it is quite unlikely that any physicalist scientist will

be impressed with this argument since prima facie it amounts to saving Psi by ac-

cepting an enormous amount of metaphysical baggage that itself is not supported

by any scientific evidence.

The Dualist Answer

     To my mind, the reply that is the most reasonable is based on a dualistic

worldview (Foster, 1991; Smythies & Beloff, 1989). That is, an ontology which

postulates that the universe contains two fundamental kinds of things -- mat-

ter/energy, and mind/consciousness. There are several kinds of such theories

(based on, for example, how these two interact, which is considered causal or pri-

mary, etc.). In the following discussion I am mainly concerned with interactionist

dualism, which posits consciousness to be an independent and causal agent (i.e.,

not an epiphenomenon) that is (usually) in some functional relationship to the

physical body of a living creature.

     Biological evolution, roughly speaking, works because certain changes in

genotype (the genetic content of the gametes) result in changes in phenotype (the

body of the organism) such that some of these changes will prove useful in certain

environmental contexts. This added usefulness results in those genes being more

efficiently spread throughout a population. This works well for characteristics that

are a direct by-product of the physical body of an organism. However, it is plausi-

ble that psi is not a function of any physical organ or structure, but rather of some

non-material consciousness (for example, Steiner, 1961). Thus, genes, that alone

are the objects of mutation, may not in themselves encode the structure/function

of the mind (though they may produce a brain that is better or worse suited for

interaction with a mind). In this way, psi abilities might be completely (or par-

tially) decoupled from evolution as there is no feedback to the genes -- there

would be no way for selection to work.

     Needless to say, though many traditions describe the evolution of the mind (or

soul), none of these systems resembles the kind of evolutionary process we asso-

ciate with physical evolution (teleologically blind alterations coupled with more

prolific reproduction of those that turn out better). Thus, there is no obvious rea-

son to expect an evolutionary process based on physical survival (which is what

we, as biologists, expect to observe as the result of the use of psi in the wild) to

exist. In fact, most theoretical dualist systems specifically mention some kind of

"conservation of souls" law (e.g., Besant, 1904, 1951), whereby souls are monadic

and indestructible (at least, over some very long time scale), and do not repro-

duce, die, or compete for limited resources in the kinds of ways that physical bod-

ies do (which is a crucial ingredient of evolution). 

     One other classical idea relevant to this argument is that of group souls

(Besant, 1951, Heindel, 1956). In this scheme, beings that have not achieved indi-

vidualization (the permanent assignment of one soul to one organism) exist as

multiple physical organisms that are "presided over" by a single group-soul or

group-mind. In this kind of scenario, one would not expect to observe evolution

     Note that in general, the dualist reply to this problem is not a resort to mystifi-

cation. It is not being claimed that there is no problem regarding evolution as psi

is non-physical and mysterious in any case.  Rather, this can be seen as a conse-

quence and a prediction of the dualist theory: dualism can be taken to imply ex-

actly this dissociation between psi and biological evolution.

Conclusion

     Clearly, dualism is a very unpopular worldview in the scientific community

today. There are, however, other arguments which support it, both related to psi

(Dilley, 1990; Levin, 1995; and references cited therein), and independent of it

(Hodgson, 1991, Lockwood, 1989, Lucas, 1961, Madell, 1988, Robinson, 1993).

Some thinkers do not find it cogent to support one dubious area of research (psi)

with an even more dubious metaphysical worldview (dualism). Others will find

that this evolutionary line of thought adds another mutually-supporting link be-

tween dualism and psi, of the kind that is provided by an analysis of psi research

that suggests that materialism is an inadequate metaphysical framework within

which to study psi phenomena (Beloff, 1985; Levin, 1995). Presumably

parapsychologists who think that psi phenomena may be explained within a mate-

rialist system (for example, Dobbs, 1967; William, 1986) would lean towards un-

usual properties of the information-carrying medium as a reply to the evolutionary

problem (as discussed above). In either case, the question of the evolution of psi

in the biosphere (or lack thereof) is one with potentially profound implications for

areas of parapsychology, philosophy, biology, and physics.
