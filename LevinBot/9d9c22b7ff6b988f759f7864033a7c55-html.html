 Introduction 

 The importance of chemical signaling via neurotrans-

mitters in the nervous system is well appreciated. Inter-
estingly, it is now becoming clear that these versatile sig-
naling molecules have important roles in morphogenesis 
[Lauder et al., 1988] as well as neural development and 
plasticity [Di Pino et al., 2004; Luo et al., 2003; Whita-
ker-Azmitia et al., 1996]. 

 Serotonin is linked to a variety of diverse medical con-

ditions including tumor growth [Loric et al., 1995; Man-
da et al., 1988; Pranzatelli and Balletti, 1992; Russo et 
al., 2003], circadian rhythm disorders [Morin, 1999], and 
cardiac disease [Deraet et al., 2005; Frishman and Gre-
wall, 2000; Nebigil and Maroteaux, 2001]. Its crucial role 
in controlling neuronal activity has allowed it to be di-
rectly implicated in both normal cognition and a number 
of syndromes such as memory impairment [Buhot et al., 
2000], sleep disorders [Jouvet, 1999], schizophrenia [Mil-
lan, 2000], dementia [Lanctot et al., 2001], autism 
[Chugani, 2002], aggression [Lesch and Merschdorf, 
2000], depression [Stockmeier, 1997], and other mood 
disorders.

 Abstract 
 Serotonin is a clinically important neurotransmitter reg-
ulating diverse aspects of cognitive function, sleep, 
mood, and appetite. Increasingly, it is becoming appreci-
ated that serotonin signaling among non-neuronal cells 
is a novel patterning mechanism existing throughout di-
verse phyla. Here, we review the evidence implicating 
serotonergic signaling in embryonic morphogenesis, in-
cluding gastrulation, craniofacial and bone patterning, 
and the generation of left-right asymmetry. We propose 
two models suggesting movement of neurotransmitter 
molecules as a novel mechanism for how bioelectrical 
events may couple to downstream signaling cascades 
and gene activation networks. The discovery of sero-
tonin-dependent patterning events occurring long be-
fore the development of the nervous system opens excit-
ing new avenues for future research in evolutionary, 
developmental, and clinical biology.

 Non-Neuronal Roles of Serotonin 

 It is now appreciated that serotonin has another crucial 

role aside from the regulation of neural activity: control 
of morphogenesis during early stages of embryogenesis, 
prior to the appearance of neurons [Buznikov et al., 2001; 
Buznikov and Shmukler, 1981; Whitaker-Azmitia et al., 
1996]. Serotonin is widely distributed in both the animal 
and the plant kingdoms and has been detected in eggs and 
oocytes of both vertebrate and invertebrate embryos [Ma-
nukhin et al., 1981; Shuey et al., 1990; Wu et al., 1999], 
as well as embryonic stem cells [Walther, 1999]. It is like-
ly to play a role in oocyte maturation [Buznikov et al., 
1993], vascular remodeling [Fanburg and Lee, 1997], 

neural crest migration [Moiseiwitsch and Lauder, 1995], 
patterning of cytoskeletal structures [Emanuelsson et al., 
1988; Lee, 1991], blastomere adhesion, gastrulation 
movements in amphibians [Hamalainen and Kohonen, 
1989],  Drosophila  [Colas et al., 1999a, b] and echino-
derms [Buznikov et al., 2001, 2005], craniofacial and 
limb development [Bhasin et al., 2004a, b; Lauder and 
Zimmerman, 1988; Shuey et al., 1992, 1993], and the 
speciﬁ cation of neuronal identity and connectivity [Lau-
der et al., 1981, 1983]. 

 The ‘Pre-Nervous’ Serotonergic System in Sea 
Urchins 

 Serotonin, as well as other classical neurotransmitters, 

are present in oocytes, early (pre-nervous) embryos, and 
larvae of all vertebrates and invertebrates studied – mam-
mals, birds, amphibians, teleosts, ascidians, echinoderms, 
insects, molluscs and nemertean worms [Buznikov, 1967, 
1990; Buznikov and Podmarev, 1990]. The most detailed 
results have been obtained for pre-nervous 5-HT in sea 
urchins [Buznikov, 1967; Buznikov et al., 2001, 1996; 
Renaud et al., 1983; Toneby, 1977]. In particular, it has 
been shown that the concentration of 5-HT (or 5-HT-like 
substances) changes in developing sea urchin embryos 
and larvae, starting from the ﬁ rst minutes after fertiliza-
tion with the synchronous changes occurring during the 
ﬁ rst cleavage divisions being the most striking [Buznikov, 
1967]. It has also been found that some 5-HT receptor 
ligands inhibit or block the ﬁ rst cleavage divisions, blas-
tulation and/or gastrulation, and that speciﬁ c antagonists 
of these ligands, as well as exogenous 5-HT and some of 
its derivatives, prevent these developmental malforma-
tions, as demonstrated using a ‘perturb-and-rescue strat-
egy’ [Buznikov et al., 2001]. It appears, therefore, that 
endogenous 5-HT is functionally active throughout pre-
nervous embryogenesis in sea urchins, mediated by 5-HT 
receptors and/or transporters. The existence of such pre-
nervous receptors (or receptor-like targets) and transport-
er (SERT), as well as 5-HT itself, has recently been con-
ﬁ rmed both pharmacologically and immunocytochemi-
cally [Buznikov et al., 2001, 2005].

 Serotonergic Regulation of Mouse 
Craniofacial Development 

 In the mouse embryo, 5-HT, derived from the mater-

nal-embryonic circulation [Yavarone et al., 1993a], acts 
as a morphogenetic signal to regulate various aspects of 
craniofacial development [reviewed by Lauder, 1988, 
1993]. During the development of the branchial arches, 
sites of 5-HT uptake and degradation are transiently ex-
pressed by epithelia [Shuey et al., 1993]. These sites ap-
pear to protect the underlying mesenchyme from expo-
sure to inappropriate levels of 5-HT, as indicated by pat-
terns of cell death and malformations, caused by exposure 
of cultured mouse embryos to selective 5-HT reuptake 
inhibitors (SSRIs), like ﬂ uoxetine (Prozac) and sertraline 
(Zoloft) [Shuey et al., 1992]. As shown in a variety of in 
vitro models, 5-HT acts as a dose-dependent growth reg-
ulatory signal for craniofacial cells, regulating neural crest 
migration [Moiseiwitsch and Lauder, 1995], mesenchy-
mal cell proliferation [Bhasin et al., 2004b; Buznikov et 
al., 2001; Nebigil et al., 2000], tooth germ development 
[Moiseiwitsch and Lauder, 1996], and expression of 
growth factors and extracellular matrix molecules, in-
cluding those of the cartilage matrix [Lambert and Lau-
der, 1999; Lambert et al., 2001; Moiseiwitsch and Lau-
der, 1997; Moiseiwitsch et al., 1998]. Serotonin carries 
out these diverse developmental functions by activating 
different 5-HT receptor subtypes [Buznikov et al., 2001; 
Lambert et al., 2001], including 5-HT 2  receptors [Bhasin 
et al., 2004a, b; Lauder et al., 2000]. Expression of 5-HT 3 

 receptors in regions of active chondrogenesis in the em-

bryonic craniofacial region [Tecott et al., 1995] suggests 
that these receptors may also play roles in craniofacial 
skeletal development. This possibility is supported by the 
ability of 5-HT 3  receptor ligands to alter expression of 
cartilage proteoglycan core protein in mandibular micro-
mass and organotypic cultures [Moiseiwitsch and Lau-
der, 1997; Moiseiwitsch et al., 1998]. The importance of 
both 5-HT 2  and 5-HT 3  receptors for embryonic craniofa-
cial development is further indicated by the potent tera-
togenetic activity of antagonists for these receptors in cul-
tured mouse embryos [Lauder et al., 1994, 2000]. 

 Serotonin Is Implicated in Bone Development 
and Homeostasis 

 Multiple 5-HT receptors (5-HT 1A , 5-HT 1D , 5-HT 2A , 

5-HT 2B ) and the 5-HT transporter (5-HTT or SERT) are 
expressed by osteoblast precursors, osteoblasts, osteo-
cytes and osteoclasts in primary cultures of bone or bone 
marrow cell lines, indicating that bone expresses a func-
tional 5-HT signaling system [Bliziotes et al., 2002, 2001; 
Warden et al., 2005a; Westbroek et al., 2001]. In primary 
bone cultures, 5-HT promotes proliferation of osteoblast 
precursors, mediated by 5-HT 2B  receptors [Westbroek et 
al., 2001], suggesting a role in bone development. This is 
supported by studies of osteogenic differentiation in an 
inducible mesoblastic cell line, C1 [Locker et al., 2005] 
showing that when cells are induced to convert to osteo-
blasts in the presence of 5-HT, they express functional 
5-HT 2B  receptors, deposit a type I collagen matrix, and 
differentiate into osteocytes. The 5-HT 2B  receptor may 
also play roles in bone homeostasis, as suggested by evi-
dence that aged female 5-HT 2B  null mutant mice exhibit 
hypomineralization and reduced bone density [Maro-
teaux, pers. commun.]. A major role of the 5-HTT, as 
studied in bone marrow cell lines, appears to be regula-
tion of bone homeostasis [Battaglino et al., 2004], al-
though it too may play roles in bone development. Mice 
with a null mutation of SERT exhibit a skeletal phenotype 
of reduced bone mass, altered bone architecture, and in-
ferior mechanical properties. This is in contrast to wild-
type mice treated postnatally with ﬂ uoxetine, which only 
exhibit deﬁ cits in bone mineralization [Warden et al., 
2005b]. Taken together, these studies suggest that a sero-
tonergic signaling system may play important roles in 
both bone development and homeostasis, and cautions 
that developmental exposure to commonly used SSRI an-
tidepressants, like Prozac, Zoloft, or Paxil, could poten-
tially affect both processes.

 Retinoic Acid (RA) and 5-HT May Regulate 
Embryonic Development as Opposing or 
Cooperative Signals 

 The question of a possible relationship between reti-

noid and serotonergic regulation of embryogenesis arose 
when studies in mouse embryos provided evidence that 
exposure of cultured embryos to pharmacologic doses of 
at-RA, or a pan-5-HT 

2   receptor antagonist, ritanserin, 

produced similar abnormal craniofacial and limb pheno-
types, leading to the suggestion that 5-HT and retinoids 
might expert opposing actions on embryonic development 
[Choi et al., 1997]. This prompted an investigation of em-
bryonic expression patterns for 5-HT   receptors and reti-
noid markers in the mouse embryo, which revealed com-
mon patterns for the 5-HT 

2B  

receptor, RALDH2 and 

CRABP1 in the craniofacial region, limbs, and heart [Bha-
sin et al., 2004b]. In support of this hypothesis, it was 
found that 5-HT promoted cell proliferation, mediated by 
5-HT 2B  receptors in organotypic cultures of the frontona-
sal mass, whereas at-RA, inhibited proliferation [Bhasin 
et al., 2004b]. Similarly, in hindlimb micromass cultures, 
stimulation of 5-HT 2B  receptors was found to promote 
chondrogenic differentiation, whereas at-RA inhibited 
this process [Bhasin et al., 2004a]. These results are con-
sistent with preliminary data in sea urchins showing that 
5-HT 2B   receptor agonists or a pan-RAR antagonist can 
rescue blastula stage sea urchin embryos from retinoid ter-
atogenesis [Buznikov and Lauder, unpubl. results]. Taken 
together, these ﬁ ndings in mice and sea urchins raise the 
possibility that the serotonergic and retinoid systems may 
act as opposing signals in various aspects of embryonic 
development. This could be relevant to regulation of left-
right (LR) patterning by retinoic acid [Chazaud et al., 
1999; Vermot and Pourquie, 2005; Zile et al., 2000]. 

 LR Asymmetry and Serotonin 

 The well-characterized cell biology of 5-HT signaling, 

combined with tantalizing data suggesting a role in im-
portant morphogenetic events well before neuronal de-
velopment, all point to the importance of characterizing 
in detail the embryonic processes which depend on 5-HT 
signaling. Serotonergic signaling has been implicated in 
large-scale axial patterning events in sea urchins [Cam-
eron et al., 1994] and  Xenopus  [Kim and Han, 1999]. 
However, the most recent and detailed insight into large-
scale patterning roles of serotonin concerns the patterning 
of the LR axis in vertebrates.  

 Invariant LR asymmetry is a fascinating feature of ver-

tebrate morphogenesis that raises key questions in cell, 
evolutionary, and developmental biology [Cooke, 2004; 
Levin, 2005]. It also has important implications for the 
biomedicine of laterality-based birth defects [Burn, 1991; 
Kosaki and Casey, 1998]. Within the last 10 years, insight 
has been gained into the molecular mechanisms which 
underlie consistent laterality of the heart and viscera dur-
ing embryonic development [Chazaud et al., 1999; Horn-
stein and Tabin, 2005; Wood, 1997; Yost, 2001]. While 
the middle phase of LR patterning (the asymmetric gene 
cascade) is fairly well understood [Levin, 1998; Whitman 
and Mercola, 2001], upstream events and their conserva-
tion among species present many open questions [Boor-
man and Shimeld, 2002; Burdine and Schier, 2000; Levin, 
2004a]. Motivated by recent ﬁ ndings indicating that the 
LR axis is patterned at very early stages by ﬂ ows of small 
molecules and ions [Levin and Mercola, 1998, 1999; 
Levin et al., 2002], we sought to examine the involvement 
of non-protein signaling molecules in mechanisms up-
stream of the known LR cascade. Seeking to discover 
mechanisms by which chiral orientation on the cellular 
level [Brown and Wolpert, 1990] may be transduced into 
the L and R positional identities of cell ﬁ elds on either 
side of the embryonic midline, Levin et al. [1998, 1999] 
previously showed that long-range signaling through gap 
junctional paths in chick and frog embryos ( ﬁ g. 2 a–c) was 
an obligate aspect of LR patterning upstream of asym-
metric gene expression [Levin and Mercola, 1998, 1999]. 
These experiments raised two main lines of inquiry: what 
controls the unidirectional (chiral) ﬂ ow of LR informa-
tion through the gap junctions [Levin et al., 2002], and 
what is the molecular nature of the small-molecule LR 
signals which are exchanged between cells on the L and 
R sides? The ideal candidate would be smaller than the 
size cut-off of gap junctions ( ! 1 kDa), be water-soluble 
(signaling molecules such as retinoic acid do not need gap 
junctions to move between cells), and be charged (to en-
able regulation of movement via ion pump-dependent 
voltage gradients [Levin, 2003b; Levin and Nascone, 
1997]). Serotonin ﬁ ts these criteria, has been demonstrat-
ed to go through gap junctions in some contexts [Wolszon 
et al., 1994], and offers the beneﬁ ts of a well-developed 
pharmacological tool set [Gaster and King, 1997].

advantage of the large number of well-characterized re-

asymmetry in chick and frog embryos. These two species

techniques and represent vertebrates with different gas-
trulation modes, Fukumoto et al. [2005a, b] explored the 
roles of serotonin in LR asymmetry. The ﬁ rst component 
was an inverse drug screen ( ﬁ g. 3 a). Frog embryos during 
a very early stage of development (fertilization to gastru-
lation) were exposed to a number of well-characterized 
drug reagents targeting all of the known components of 
the 5-HT pathway: receptors, synthesis and degradation 
machinery, and transporters. The screen assayed for spe-

ciﬁ c reversals of the positioning of the heart, gut, and gall 
bladder ( ﬁ g. 3 b) in the absence of other defects (especial-
ly in DV and AP patterning). Using this screening meth-
odology, which has previously been used to identify LR-
relevant ion ﬂ ows [Adams and Levin, 2005; Levin et al., 
2002], it was possible to rapidly and inexpensively test 
the hypothesis that 5-HT was an endogenous component 
of the LR pathway and to implicate speciﬁ c molecular 
candidates for further functional analysis. 

 The components that were positively implicated in 

both species were receptor subtypes 3 and 4, the SERT 
transporter, and degradation via MAO ( ﬁ g. 3 c). Manipu-
lation of any of these components resulted in heterotaxia 
(an independent and random positioning of the heart and 
viscera). Interestingly, manipulation of R3 activity led 
mostly to complete situs inversus, a very rare phenotype 
in the LR ﬁ eld [Hyatt and Yost, 1998]. Timing and epis-
tasis experiments indicated that these functioned up-
stream of the correct laterality of the earliest asymmetric 
genes (Nodal in frog [Lohr et al., 1998; Lowe et al., 1996], 
and Sonic hedgehog in chick [Levin et al., 1995]).  

 Using misexpression of dominant-negative and w.t. 

constructs, and an amine-binding protein [Andersen et 
al., 2003], functional molecular experiments conﬁ rmed 
the implication of 5-HT signaling by the drug screen. The 
analysis of endogenous localization proved highly infor-
mative as well; all of the targets implicated were present 
in frog embryos from fertilization (with the exception of 
MAO which was upregulated after the ﬁ rst few cleavages) 
and in chick embryos from the initiation of the primitive 
streak, conﬁ rming the serotonin pathway as a regulatory 
step which can function long prior to the appearance of 
neuronal cells. In the chick, MAO and SERT proved to 
be asymmetrically localized in Hensen’s node, adding to 
the available markers of asymmetry [Levin, 2005]. Im-
portantly, it was ﬁ rst necessary to characterize available 
serotonin antibodies [Levin, 2004b]; two commercial an-
tibodies sold as 5-HT speciﬁ c in fact do not differentiate 
well between serotonin and related molecules. In  Xeno-
pus , serotonin was distributed in a striking radial pattern, 
eventually forming a gradient and coalescing into a single 
blastomere on one side of the midline by the 32/64-cell 
stage ( ﬁ g. 3 d, e). In the chick embryo, serotonin was local-
ized throughout the streak, consistent with older radio-
graphic [Emanuelsson et al., 1988] and ﬂ uorescence his-
tochemical and immunocytochemical data [Wallace, 
1982]. The asymmetric catalytic activity of MAO is like-
ly to provide differential 5-HT levels on the right vs. left 
sides of Hensen’s node, but this has not been demonstrat-
ed by immunohistochemistry.

 HPLC analysis revealed that serotonin was present as 

a maternal pool in eggs prior to fertilization in frog em-
bryos. Moreover, it demonstrated a progressive MAO-
dependent drop of total 5-HT level that was crucial for 
correct LR asymmetry. These data are consistent with the 
lack of importance of 5-HT synthesis for asymmetry in 
 Xenopus . Importantly, R3, R4, and SERT were necessary 
somewhat earlier than MAO, consistent with the hypoth-
esis that MAO serves to limit the available serotonin pool 
after translocation and signaling events have occurred. 
Interestingly, the chick embryo is different in that it zy-
gotically synthesizes 5-HT, and this synthesis is required 
for asymmetry. Thus, the data in chick and frog reveal 
similarities and differences. Both systems use 5-HT as 
part of an early LR signaling step; both rely on MAO, R3, 
R4, and SERT. However, frog embryos operate with a 
maternal pool of serotonin which is to be degraded by the 
blastula stage, while chick embryos utilize zygotically syn-
thesized serotonin during gastrulation. In the frog, 5-HT 
localization is dependent on gap-junctional communica-
tion (GJC) and H,K-ATPase activity, adding weight to 
the possibility that serotonin movement occurs through 
gap junctions and is driven by electrophoretic forces gen-
erated by ion pump-dependent membrane voltage differ-
ences across the ventral midline [Levin, 2004a]. 

 These data shed light on new elements of LR pattern-

ing and new aspects of serotonergic control in morpho-
genesis. However, a number of important questions re-
main. First, the chick data do not directly support a GJC 
morphogen model for 5-HT function because asymmet-
ric localization of 5-HT itself has not been demonstrated 
in this species. Moreover, no simple models explain the 
randomization of Shh expression in the node following 
5-HT perturbation. Finally, the available chick data con-
cern macroscopic (embryo-wide) localization. Therefore, 
sub-cellular investigation of 5-HT movement as well as 
sensitive comparisons between the two sides of Hensen’s 
node will be required to understand the role of SERT-
mediated transport and 5-HT signaling in detail. 

 Moreover, in the frog, direct movement of 5-HT in 

vivo has not been demonstrated. Unfortunately, given 
the small size of serotonin, current technology does not 
permit labeling it in such a way as to allow in vivo analy-
sis of its movement; all available tags which are compat-
ible with detection in living cells alter its molecular weight 
by an order of magnitude, likely signiﬁ cantly altering its 
interaction with endogenous binding partners, as well as 
the rate and extent of its movement. New techniques will 
need to be developed so that serotonin movement can be 
tracked in living embryos. 

 Future Prospects 

 The available data on morphogenetic functions for se-

rotonin are only the tip of the iceberg and many oppor-
tunities for important discovery now present themselves. 
Patterning roles of neurotransmitter signals reveal fasci-
nating aspects of evolutionary developmental biology. 
The serotonergic synapse has been suggested to be an evo-
lutionary co-opting of far more primitive signaling events 
between embryonic blastomeres [Buznikov, 1984; Buz -
nikov et al., 1996; Buznikov and Shmukler, 1981]. The 
conservation of serotonin in LR patterning beyond frog 
and chick embryos remains to be determined, and it is 
not known whether it applies to mammals as well. The 
possibility must be considered that other neurotransmit-
ters may substitute for serotonin in other species (and 
indeed may represent keys to uncovering novel pattern-
ing events). Indeed, experiments in rodent embryos sug-
gest the involvement of adrenergic signals in LR pattern-
ing in mammals [Fujinaga and Baden, 1991; Fujinaga et 
al., 1990], and GABA appears to regulate growth in  Te-
trahymena  [Castrodad et al., 1988; Darvas et al., 1985]. 
Drug screens similar to that performed by Fukumoto et 
al. [2005b] provide an attractive area for identifying oth-
er neurotransmitter compounds with novel patterning 
functions. 

 It will be key to integrate 5-HT signaling with down-

stream gene expression. We have begun array analysis of 
embryos following extracellular and intracellular regula-
tion (both loss and gain of function) of 5-HT signaling in 
frog embryos. Similar efforts in other systems will iden-
tify early response genes which link activation of sero-
tonin receptors and intracellular systems to downstream 
cascades. Interestingly, signiﬁ cant insight for the sero-
tonin ﬁ eld could come from the current advances in plant 
systems. Auxin, a plant hormone that bears a striking 
similarity to serotonin, is a positional signal in a number 
of plant patterning events [Baluska et al., 2003; Barlow, 
2005; Friml, 2003; Sabatini et al., 1999; Vroemen et al., 
1999] and is involved in establishment of bilateral sym-
metry in plants [Lee and Evans, 1985; Liu et al., 1993; 
Zgurski et al., 2005]. Indeed, many of the same players 
that have been implicated in LR asymmetry (K 

+  chan-

nels, plasma membrane H 

+   ﬂ ux, cell membrane trans-

porters and gradients of serotonin/auxin, regulatory roles 
of pH, etc.) are now known to be crucial components of 
auxin signaling [Arend et al., 2002; Coenen et al., 2002; 
Hager, 2003; Pasternak et al., 2002; Rober-Kleber et al., 
2003; Wind et al., 2004].

 The ability of 5-HT to localize to a speciﬁ c blastomere 

depends on gap junctional communication and H,K-
ATPase function, which (in concert with a potassium 
channel) may provide an electron motive force for mov-
ing charged small molecules (such as serotonin) between 
cells [Levin, 2004a]. Conversely, a number of ion trans-
porters are controlled by 5-HT, most notably R3 and 
SERT [Maricq et al., 1991; Quick, 2003]. As this ﬁ eld 
matures, it will be necessary to develop quantitative mod-
els of the bidirectional relationship between 5-HT move-
ment in an electric ﬁ eld and the regulation of ion trans-
porters by 5-HT. The activation of asymmetric ion ﬂ ux 
by serotonin and the unidirectional movement of 5-HT 
due to an electrophoretic force may thus be a positive-
feedback loop that could magnify small asymmetries on 
the cellular level into asymmetry on the scale of cell ﬁ elds. 
The movement may be intracellular or intercellular in 
various patterning systems. Indeed, plant root tips set up 
a sink-driven gradient of auxin [Friml et al., 2002]; the 
serotonin gradient in frog embryos is likewise dependent 
on the degradation machinery. A model has been pro-
posed [Paponov et al., 2005] where auxin ﬂ ow, starting 
by diffusion, induces formation of the polar transport sys-
tem. This in turn promotes auxin transport, leading to 
canalization of auxin ﬂ ow along a narrow column of cells 
and ultimately controlling differentiation. A similar am-
pliﬁ cation loop between 5-HT and K 

+  ﬂ ow through R3 is 

precisely the kind of mechanism which has been pro-
posed to account for the function of 5-HT movement 
during LR patterning [Fukumoto et al., 2005b]. The in-
volvement of electrophoretic mechanisms relevant to se-
rotonin-like molecules in plant and animal patterning 
[Fischer et al., 1997; Goldsworthy and Rathore, 1985; 
Palmgren, 1998; Rober-Kleber et al., 2003] suggest that 
fascinating aspects of evolutionary conservation of such 
patterning mechanisms await investigation. 

 Interestingly, previous studies have indicated the pres-

ence of cytoplasmic serotonergic signaling mechanisms 
[Bhatnagar et al., 2001; Buznikov et al., 1993, 2005; 
Buznikov and Shmukler, 1981; Fiorica-Howells et al., 
2002; Xia et al., 2003] in addition to the well-character-
ized canonical cell-membrane receptors. The modulation 
of proliferation of fetal heart cells by 5-HT and effects on 
embryonic morphogenesis are dependent on SERT func-
tion [Eddahibi et al., 2001; Gaspar et al., 2003; Persico et 
al., 2000, 2001; Sari and Zhou, 2003; Shuey et al., 1992; 
Yavarone et al., 1993b], suggesting the possibility that the 
internal concentration of serotonin may be an important 
parameter. This in turn implies the presence of an intracel-
lular receptor for 5-HT. Serotonin has been detected in the 

nucleus [Csaba and Barath, 1974; Csaba and Sudar, 1978; 
Emanuelsson, 1992; Emanuelsson et al., 1988; Sudar and 
Csaba, 1979; Yavarone et al., 1993a], and can bind DNA 
[Hadi et al., 2001] as well as cytoskeletal structures [Ema-
nuelsson et al., 1988; Lee, 1991]. Implicated mechanisms 
also include serotonylation of small proteins [Walther 
et al., 2003] and intracellular serotonin-binding proteins 
 [Jimenez Del Rio, 1992, 1993; Rio et al., 1995; Tamir and 
Gershon, 1981; Tamir et al., 1994], although they remain 
uncharacterized at the molecular genetic level. 

 A novel cytoplasmic locus for 5-HT signaling was sup-

ported by the recent studies of serotonin in asymmetry, 
where it was found that laterality was randomized by ma-
nipulation of intracellular serotonin signaling (via intra-
cellular introduction of a serotonin-binding protein which 
is known to inactivate 5-HT [Andersen et al., 2003], or 
microinjection of excess serotonin directly into blasto-
meres). Cytoplasmic signaling by 5-HT could occur 
through an intracellular serotonin-binding protein, or 
through modulation of one of the known receptors if it 
were active in the cytoplasm [Mialet et al., 2003; Whalen 
et al., 2000]. Recent evidence has suggested that some 5-
HT receptors are active while inside the cell. These recep-
tors appear to be of the 5-HT 2  receptor (R2) family [Bhat-
nagar et al., 2001; Cornea-Hebert et al., 1999; Doherty and 
Pickel, 2000; Fiorica-Howells et al., 2002; Riad et al., 2001; 
Xia et al., 2003]. However, this has not yet been reported 
for R3 or R4. The possibility that serotonin is a small mor-
phogen which traverses endogenous gap junctional paths 
during LR patterning requires intracellular receptor mech-
anisms for serotonin arriving inside the target cell through 
gap junctions (see below). Interesting new pathways are 
likely to be discovered by future efforts geared towards the 
identiﬁ cation and characterization of novel cytoplasmic 
serotonin receptors existing within embryos.

ing may shed light on another interesting issue in devel-

embryonic development and regeneration [Jaffe, 1982; 
Levin, 2003a; McCaig et al., 2005; Nuccitelli, 2003a, b; 
Robinson and Messerli, 2003]. The mechanisms by which 
gradients created by cells and epithelia exert effects on 
neighboring cells are unclear, although a number of pro-
posals have been put forward [McCaig et al., 2002; Rob-
inson, 1985]. The serotonin data arising from studies of 
LR asymmetry in chick and frog are compatible with two 
broad (and not mutually exclusive) models: electrophore-
