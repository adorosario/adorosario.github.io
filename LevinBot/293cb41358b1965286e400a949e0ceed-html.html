Introduction

In this review, I would like to present the idea that electromagnetic

fields are carriers of biologically-important information. This has been

amply shown for chemical messengers, but the mainstream biology

community largely ignores the role of endogenous EM fields in controlling

biosystems (with the exception of neuronal conduction). Many properties of

biological systems, such as polarity, long-range spatial order, and positional

information are present in the physics of electromagnetic fields. Here, I

review some evidence that endogenous DC electric fields, magnetic fields,

and ultra-weak photon emission are part of the medium by which

information flows in biological systems. Due to space constraints, I will

cite only sample references; there are many more relevant studies available

for each point than I could possibly cite in this review.

Electromagnetic fields are currently used in medicine (Bassett,

1993; O'Connor et al., 1990; Pilla and Markov, 1994). An excellent general

The L-field Theory

Bio-information transfer figures prominently in ecology and animal

communication (Presman, 1970; Becker, 1976). I would like to focus on

spatial pattern, and the possibility that the morphology (embryonic

293cb41358b1965286e400a949e0ceed-html.html

geometry) of organisms is encoded in the action of endogenous

electromagnetic fields. This idea has been set out in various forms.

Lund (1947) conducted an extensive series of experiments which

correlated changes in natural electric fields with development and

regeneration and showed for the first time that externally applied electric

fields can affect morphogenesis of various organisms. The L-field theory

(most explicitly formulated by Burr and Northrop, 1937) holds that a

complex electromagnetic field present within living organisms is a key

factor in morphogenesis and contains part of the information needed to

produce a 3-dimensional organism. This overlapping set of fields of various

types is referred to as the L-field.  Formulations of this field in terms of

currents or potential gradients are equivalent and inter-convertible. Needless

to say a cell's chemical characteristics alter  its response to the field. There is

continuous feedback,  the field altering cell behavior, which in turn alters

field  characteristics (Burr and Northrop, 1939).

In full generality, this theory stresses the importance of

electromagnetic communication between cells, both in terms of electric

fields and electromagnetic radiation.  Electromagnetic fields are seen as a

basic and essential feature to the control of biological processes and the

organization of complex biosystems. At this stage of our knowledge, we can

only concentrate on  mapping the field as individual currents or contour

maps of potential differences, and investigating externally-applied field

293cb41358b1965286e400a949e0ceed-html.html

effects  on cells and tissues. The ultimate goal is the formulation of a real

field model of development which  would be based upon actual EM

measurements, i.e. a mathematical  description of the total field which is

present in an organism.  For example, Larter and Ortoleva (1981) present a

detailed mathematical model of natural electric fields functioning as

patterning mechanisms in early development (this excellent paper also

discusses information storage, symmetry conservation and breaking, and

non-linear stochastic mechanisms as they apply to an electrically-controlled

self-organizing system).

Burr (1937; Burr and Hovland, 1937a; Burr et al., 1938c) found

correlations between many biological processes (ovulation, cancer,

development, etc.) and specific changes in the organism's electrical

characteristics. Burr formulated the field concept in terms of standing

potential differences. Jaffe developed the vibrating probe, allowing for

measurements of extremely weak bioelectric fields, in real-time, in living

organisms. "The fundamental basis of this theory is that the pattern of

organization of any biological system is established by a complex electro-

dynamical field which is in part determined by its atomic physico-chemical

components and which in part determines the behavior and orientation  of

those components. This field is electrical in the physical sense." (Northrop

and Burr, 1937; Burr, 1944a). There is now much more support for this

293cb41358b1965286e400a949e0ceed-html.html

theory than when Burr first proposed it. See Jaffe, 1981 for a more modern

formulation of these ideas.

In the rest of this paper, I examine three aspects of biology which deal

directly with morphological information. During embryogenesis, a

developing organism must achieve, within fairly tight parameters, a very

particular morphology of external form and internal organization, from

organelles all the way to the whole organism. The process of regeneration

illustrates the maintenance and restoration of that morphology in light of

environmental injury. Finally, cancer illustrates the consequences of some

part of the organism ignoring the large-scale morphological plan and

growing outside of its control. In discussing the role of EM fields in these

three types of processes, I will briefly go over correlations of endogenous

EM field characteristics with relevant biological events, as well as specific

changes caused by externally applied fields. Together, these two lines of

evidence show that EM fields are an integral part of information flow in the

process.

Patterning fields in regeneration

Regeneration is a special case of morphogenesis, since it occurs in an

adult organism.  In replacing a lost bodypart, embryonic developmental

mechanisms must be recruited to restore pattern. Some animals normally

exhibit regeneration (for example, tails and whole limbs); others do not.  It is

293cb41358b1965286e400a949e0ceed-html.html

important to note that even animals which are not normally known for their

regenerative ability can regenerate in special cases.  For example, human

children will regenerate severed fingertips if the stump is not pulled over

with skin (Illingworth, 1974; Borgens, 1982; Illingworth and Barker, 1980).

The regenerating limb system in amphibians has an electrical control

component, including electrically-mediated de-differentiation and axial

control (Becker and Murray, 1967b; Becker, 1972; Borgens, 1984a; Borgens

et al., 1979d). This idea is based on the findings that 1) endogenous EM

fields exist in regenerating systems, 2) there are differences between

regenerating and non-regenerating animals’ field characteristics, 3)

disruption of endogenous fields by shunting inhibits regeneration, and 4)

application of exogenous fields is able to alter regeneration and even induce

it in normally non-regenerating species. This data is summarized in Table 3:

When a limb is amputated, an injury current appears, which is thought

to induce de-differentiation into, or activation of, blastema cells. It further

serves to pattern the limb forming from these cells by attracting neuronal

growth and providing spatial information for cells migrating into the new

limb.

An interesting relationship between electrical measurements and

regeneration can be observed in the segmented earthworm (Kurtz and

Schrank, 1955, Moment, 1946, 1949). Wherever the worm is cut, new

segments are added until there are about 90 segments. The number of

segments is controlled by electrical potential: each segment has a voltage,

and segments are added until the overall voltage totals some value.

One of the most exciting series of experiments has shown that

electrical fields can induce regeneration in normally non-regenerating

species (Smith, 1974). For example, minute, steady, electrical fields imposed

within forelimb stumps of adult frogs initiated limb regeneration (Smith,

1979). Becker and Sparado (1972, Becker, 1972) have produced partial limb

regeneration in mammals using an applied electric field.

Shunt experiments (disturbing the natural fields) provide a way to test

the causal importance of the natural currents in regeneration.  Short-

circuiting the endogenous fields (by means of ionic depletion of the medium,

skin flaps, or even with conducting wires) results in a cessation of

regeneration (Borgens et al., 1979c,d, Borgens, 1982). This is evidence that

the currents are of prime importance in regeneration. It has been argued that

frogs do not regenerate limbs because they possess a very loose skin which

overlays large subdermal lymph spaces; urodeles (regenerating salamanders)

do not.  These lymph spaces may serve as shunts (low-resistance paths)

which short-circuit the current, thus interfering with the currents' role in

regeneration (Borgens et al., 1979b)

Conclusion

While individual workers focus on specific mechanistic measurements

of endogenous EM fields, or effects caused by exogenous field exposure, it

is profitable to take a wider look and consider the body of work as a whole. I

would argue that there is much evidence in regarding EM field interactions

with organisms as information, rather than simple mechanical influence. One

set of data that supports this view is the observation that to achieve the same

effects, greater magnetic fields must be used on individual cells and tissues

than on the whole organism (Barnothy, 1964). This points to an

amplification effect, which manifests itself as a systems property - it appears

with increasing organizational complexity (Adey, 1980). Presman (1970)

summarizes: "it has been found that entire organisms are most sensitive to

EMFs, isolated organs and cells less, and solutions of macromolecules are

even less sensitive" (p. 8). "The appearance of enhanced sensitivity to EMFs

only in fairly complexly organized biological systems can be regarded as

293cb41358b1965286e400a949e0ceed-html.html

one of the manifestations of the specific nature of life - its 'organization'" (p.

A strong case can be made that the current emphasis on small-scale

and readily-testable predictions. Future work on informational aspects of

endogenous electric and magnetic fields, and ultraweak photon emission will

be crucial in elucidating the nature and origin of high-level morphogenetic

control in growth and development of biosystems. This will have enormous

implications for human medicine as well as basic understanding of biology.
