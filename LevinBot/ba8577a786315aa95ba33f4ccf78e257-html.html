A computational model of planarian regeneration

Abhishek Dea, V. Srinivasa Chakravarthyb and Michael Levinc 

aProgram in neuroscience, university of Washington, seattle, Wa, usa; bComputational neuroscience laboratory,

ABSTRACT

Regeneration of complex anatomical structures is an emergent phenomenon 

arising from complex interplay between various underlying cellular 

components and processes. It is still largely unclear how coordination 

among cells leads to accurate regeneration of the organism, preserving the 

location, shape, and composition of the parts with respect to the whole. 

Here, we examine at the global interaction of cells in a computational model 

of planarian regeneration. A key feature of our model is the integration 

of multiple organizational levels of an organism – from cells, to network, 

to global shape. The computational model is able to replicate most of 

the experimental observations thereby facilitating study of the putative 

mechanisms. We observe that a hierarchical interplay between local and 

long range signaling acts as a positional map that guides the cellular fate at 

any position. Furthermore, we have quantified the quality of regeneration 

using a metric that provides a sense of how well the regenerated organism 

resembles its original shape.

Planaria are complex bilaterian animals with a remarkable capability. They 

regenerate after almost any sort of surgical damage, re-growing precisely 

the needed structures at the correct locations and stopping growth and 

remodeling after their target morphology is complete. Understanding the 

dynamic regulation of biological patterning is key to transformative advances 

in regenerative medicine and synthetic bioengineering. Here, we propose 

a connectionist model of pattern memory during planarian regeneration.

1. Introduction
In recent years, planarian flatworms have established themselves as a popular model organism for 
studying regeneration [12,20,23,26,31]. existing planarian regeneration models [29] combine posi-
tional information and cell migration to explain how planarians can restore Anterior–Posterior (AP) 
polarity, whereas others use the Reaction-Diffusion framework [32,34] to explain the regeneration of 
correct axial polarity. Some of the models assume an innate coordinate system that facilitates pat-
terning and are based on bioelectrical signals [37], dorsal-ventral interactions [16,17], or intercalation 
of missing regions [1]. However, none of the above models explain all the main results of planarian 
experiments. More broadly, there is a dire need of a single framework that can explain the ability of 
planaria to restore correct anatomical polarity, shape, and size despite a wide range of damage and 
other perturbations.

In our current work, we have borrowed tools from Cellular Automata [35] and Artificial Neural 

Networks [15] for building a computational model at an organism level so as to understand how local 
interactions among cells guided by chemical and electrical coupling can orchestrate planarian regen-
eration. Our regeneration model starts with an a priori mature planarian, which is then amputated at 
different locations and levels to observe if the planarian is able to regenerate back to its original shape 
and stabilize. The dynamics of regeneration from this simplistic model provides a window of cognition 
onto the underlying mechanism through which we can study the local and global cellular dynamics of 
regenerative coordination. We find that our model is able to capture most of the experimental obser-
vations on Planarian regeneration [20] and provide some key insights into the biological algorithm 
underlying regeneration.

2. Results
We start our simulation with a fully developed planarian as shown in Figure 1A. The model is simulated 
in a total space of 80 × 80 pixels. This space represents the external medium where the tissue/planarian 
is cultured. The mature planarian has a horizontal length of 15 pixels along the east–west axis and a 
vertical length of 30 pixels along the north–south axis. The planarian tissue has two main components, 
namely the ‘neurons’ that belong to the nervous system and the ‘non-neuronal cells’ which are not the 
part of nervous system. An important point to note is that, this distinction is an assumption of the 
model and is specifically motivated to distinguish neural signaling from non-neuronal signaling. Also, 
these non-neuronal cells should not be confused with the glial cells which have often been labeled as 
non-neuronal cells [13]. The ‘non-neuronal cells’ will be referred to as ‘cells’ henceforth.

each cell has five different state variables which it has to compute and remember at each iteration. 

These state variables are x

g (growth variable), xp (patterning variable), xc1 (east–west morphogen var-

iable), x

c2 (north–south morphogen variable) and xa (apoptosis variable) and resemble the activity of 

different genes encoded within a cell. For example: x

g represents the activity of a single gene coding 

the machinery of aging in a cell, x

c1 represents the activity of a gene coding for how much of an east–

west morphogen a cell should secrete etc. The east–west and north–south morphogen variables act as 
positional cues indirectly providing information about the spatial location of each cell. The morphogen 
value of each cell is influenced by three factors – local cell-cell interaction (forces the cell to secrete a 
particular morphogen concentration), gap-junctional coupling (modulates the flow of morphogens 
between a cell and its neighbors based on concentration gradient) and cell-neuron interaction (refer 
Appendix Section 1.4 for mathematical details). The mature planarian in our model has a smooth linear 
morphogen gradient along both east–west and north–south direction. The conventions: east–west and 
north–south indicate two orthogonal directions in which the morphogen gradients are considered.

We define two 1-D neural skeletons comprising of neurons (Figure 1) each modulating east–west 

and north–south morphogen activity by interacting with the cells. each cell receives projection from 
only two neurons, one from each neural skeleton. The neuron belonging to the east–west skeleton pro-
vides inputs that modulates the east–west morphogen concentration of the cell. Similarly, the neuron 
belonging to the north–south skeleton provides input, necessary for shaping the north–south mor-
phogen concentration of that cell. The connectivity between neurons and cells along the east–west 
axis is highlighted in Figure 1b.

These two neural systems work independently of each other. A prime motivation behind this imple-

mentation comes from [23] where it has been established that long-range neural signaling provides 
polarity cues during planarian regeneration [18]. In our model, each ‘neuron’ (and not a cell) has two 
important variables: a genetic element (denoted by λ) encoding the east–west and north–south morph-
ogen gradient (slope) and its own membrane potential. At each iteration, the neuron has to compute its 
own membrane potential. This membrane potential and λ are sensitive to external stimuli; for example 
an external electric field. As the neurons are connected to the cells, any change in the external stimuli 
affects the membrane potential of the neurons and λ. This change in turn causes a change in input to 
cells from a neuron by feedback. The input to a cell from a neuron controls the secretion of morphogens. 
Therefore, any change in the external environment indirectly affects the morphogen concentration 
via the activity of the neurons and ultimately modifies the morphogen gradient which is tied to the 
shape of planarian (refer Appendix Section 1.6). Thus, this model has a hierarchical architecture (Figure 
1b) in which the nervous system (global) controls the existing cellular (local) network computing the 
morphogen concentration. Further details about the model are present in Appendix.

2.1. Regeneration

Regeneration of an amputated portion of a planarian is modeled as follows. In order to regenerate the 
lost part of its body, the growth variable (x

g) of two rows of cells just near the ‘wounded’ border is set 

to a lower value (0.5). This represents the physiological migration of stem cells called ‘neoblasts’ near 
the wound where they aggregate to form a blastema which initiates the tissue to regenerate [20]. Such 
migration facilitates growth at the site of the wound. This growth is undirected and forms an omnidirec-
tional blob around the wounded border. Not only does the tissue grow but the neurons in the neural 
skeleton increase in number accordingly (refer Appendix Section 1.7). As the morphogen gradients 
re-establish, the values of x

c

1,

x

c

2 and x

a together prune back the planarian to its actual genetically 

encoded shape. The quality of regeneration was quantified using a metric named ‘power of regeneration’ 
(PoR). It was defined as the ratio of the overlapped area between original and regenerated planarian 
to the area (space) covered by original and regenerated planarian combined together (mathematically 
termed as union of the original and the regenerated planarian). Ideally, 0 represents a bad regeneration 
and 1 represents perfect regeneration. Shown below is the mathematical equation describing PoR.

Six different cuts were made to observe the ability of the network to grow itself back to its original shape. 
The cut was made at iteration 1 and the simulation was run till iteration 170 to obtain the regenerated 
planarian. In all of the experiments, we observed that the neuronal network guided the tissue to grow 
back its original shape. The regenerated planarian did exhibit left–right asymmetry, anterior posterior 
polarity and reversal of the morphogen gradient on the application of a reverse electric field. each result 
is subdivided into four subfigures. The leftmost figure displays the portions of planarian that were cut 
(displayed in green) in order to observe regeneration. The subsequent figures display the regeneration 
of planarian in a chronological order.

2.2.  Lateral regeneration

In Figure 3, the left portion of planarian about the vertical or north–south axis is cut which is shown in 
the leftmost subfigure in green color. The premature cells to the left of the pruned region are allowed 
to grow. Apoptosis takes place if their morphogen values exceed the bound which actually defines the 
boundary of the shape of planarian. The final shape of the planarian is given by the rightmost figure. 
PoR was found to be 0.55. Though the overall shape (rocket shaped) of the regrown planarian is similar 
to the initial shape, the regenerated planarian is smaller in length and larger width, which is the cause 
of a low value of PoR.

In the next experiment shown in Figure 4, a fraction of right portion of planarian along the east–west 

axis is cut. The leftmost figure shows where and how the incision is made. The rightmost figure is the 
final form of the planarian achieved in the simulation. PoR is 0.74. The regenerated planarian has an 
extension on its lower right edge and the size is slightly larger by 3–4 pixels in width as compared to 
the initial planarian.

2.3.  Tail regeneration

The lower region of the planarian extending beyond the 50th row from the top is removed. As the 
morphogen has a linear gradient along north–south axis, the cut was decided on a morphogen value 
that lies somewhere between the lowest (x

c2 = 0.30) and the highest morphogen values (xc2 = 0.70) 

along north–south axis. The reddish shade of the growth variable present on the left figure highlights 
that their age is less compared to the cells that are brown in color. The cells just above the wound have 
their cell division property restored. This is done by decreasing the growth variables of the cells (from 
1.0 to 0.60) so that the planarian grows back to its original shape and size. It is evident from the final 
subfigure that regenerated shape resembles the original one except that the tail is slightly asymmetri-
cally extended to the left. PoR is 0.55. This lowering of PoR is because of the mismatch of the head and 
extended tail between the original and regenerated planarian.

2.4.  Head regeneration

The upper portion of the planarian lying within the 35th row from the top is cut off which is shown

2.5.  Head and tail regeneration

In the following experiment, we were interested in exploring if both the head and the tail of planarian 
were simultaneously able to regenerate after they have been cut (Figure 7). The planarian cells lying 
above the 35th row and below the 50th row were eliminated. One would expect that the model 
would yield back the head and the tail. but that does not specifically highlight the robustness of the 
network computing the morphogen values. In order to understand the robustness of the morphogen 
computation, the east–west and north–south morphogen values of the remaining cells (after the 
head and the tail were cut) are reinitialized to the mean value of the morphogen concentration of 
all the cells alive. In other words, the morphogen values of the cells were distorted and the planarian 
was allowed to grow. The right-most subfigure is the final regrown planarian. Here, the model is able 
to regrow the planarian’s head and tail. This result is consistent across multiple trials. PoR for this 
experiment was 0.81.

2.6.  Regeneration under reverse external electric field

In this experiment, not only were the head and the tail of planarian cut, but also an external electric 
field was applied such that the direction of the applied electric field is opposite to the direction of the 
existing north–south morphogen gradient. It should be kept in mind that in this model, the neurons 
in addition to interacting among themselves are also interacting with the cells continuously. So if any 
external change which influences the neural activity will eventually modify the cell function (morphogen 
secretion). For example, let’s consider a situation where there are five neurons in the skeleton, with the 
first neuron having the lowest electrical activity and the last neuron having the largest electrical activity. 
Now when an electric field is applied in the direction opposite to the existing gradient of neural activity, 
the slope/gradient of the neural activity will change such that the first neuron will have the highest 
electrical activity and the last neuron will have the lowest electrical activity [21,22,33]. based on this 
theory, we hypothesize that this reverse electric field will reverse the north–south morphogen gradient 
and result in the generation of tail in the place of head and vice versa. It will essentially perform a reversal 
of the body plan under the reversal of an external electric field. Although, this hypothesis emerges from 
the experimental results [20], we were particularly interested in understanding what factors need to be 
altered in order to bring about such a change. Simulation results show that our hypothesis might be 
true. Not only is the model able to reverse the body plan (Figure 8) but also provide an intuition about 
how long range and short range signaling mechanisms coordinate to give rise to a body plan based 
on the neurons’ electrical activity and the cells’ chemical communication.

3. Discussion
We presented a computational model with the aim of explaining the role of network computation in 
regeneration. The model starts with a fully-grown planarian where the genetic code within the cells is 
stored in the form of various parameters (refer Table 1 and Appendix). The expression of this code is seen 
in the form of variables that define each cell’s state. The coordination of cellular processes provides a

to understand how emergence arises in a quantitative manner.

The model provides a new perspective on the role of neighborhood (local) interaction and long-

range (neural) interactions during regeneration. These computations implement a powerful paradigm 
for studying and understanding regeneration. The model in the current form uses rather abstract rep-
resentations for cell state and interactions, which need to be further expanded to detailed biophysical 
and biochemical mechanisms in future versions of the model.

A recent modeling attempt [4,30] shares a similar spirit in providing a conceptual framework for 

regeneration based on local and global signals for establishing a pattern based on cellular memory. 
Our model differs from their framework in numerous aspects. The global signal in our model mimics 
the neural signaling rather than stem cell chemical secretion [4]. The pattern formation in our model 
involves the participation of all neurons and non-neuronal cells as opposed to an involvement from 
only specialized stem cells [4]. The pattern in our model is dependent on a network of cells secreting 
morphogens along two directions and gap-junctional coupling rather than a comparison between 
a stored radially decaying signal distribution and the total signal received from its neighboring cells. 
[4,30]. It is also uncertain if their model can mimic the effects of reversal of applied electric field which 
is successfully demonstrated by our model.

Overall, our model provides a mechanistic and algorithmic understanding of planarian regeneration. 

The simulation results also do bring out some important observations which are in general difficult to 
quantify using wet-lab experiments:

(1)     The model shows that the strength of the local interactions among the cells could be a strong 

factor in determining the overall global shape of an organism.

(2)     The model confirmed the ability of the morphogen signaling to act as a positional cue and 

determine the cellular fate.

(3)     The model demonstrated the regenerative mechanisms of planarian. It was able to shed light 

on how stem cell activity near the wound can trigger the regeneration mechanism.

(4)     gap junction communications could be one of the possible mechanisms responsible for 

smoothening the gradient of the morphogens (see Appendix Section 1.4).

Although our model brings forward some of the important facets of planarian regeneration, it still 

has few gaps that need to be filled. From the simulation (Figures 3–8), it might appear that pruning 
happens after the tissue has grown to a considerable size which might seem biologically unrealistic. but 
the equations that govern our model perform both pruning and growth computation simultaneously. 
It is just that the influence of pruning is weaker and slower than growth, that during the early regen-
erative stages in the model that it is hardly visible. At later regenerative stages, the effect of pruning 
increases and becomes manifest.

The original form was poorly restored when the extent of injury was significant. This is evident in 

Figure 9 which shows that, as the extent of injury is increased, the PoR is decreased. One possible reason 
for this outcome could be attributed to the distortions introduced in the positioning system (morpho-
gen gradients) due to extensive injury. Since the cellular fate is based on this positional information, a 
serious damage to the positional system distorts the global shape. During such circumstances, one can 
artificially use constraints on the amount of morphogen a cell can secrete at each position but doing 
so would make the regeneration not rely on cell–cell, cell–neuron and gap junction interactions which 
is a crucial component of the model. Moreover the relevance of dynamical morphogen computation 
will be diminished.

The phenomenon of cell polarity and cell movement was neglected in order to simplify the model. 

Planar polarity is now known to be a key property for morphogenesis [10,36] and regeneration specif-
ically [3,27]. In our model, the cells were represented as single units with no specific polarity. In other 
words, the directionality of the organism as a whole was defined but the polarity of a single cell was 
neglected. Cell movement [6] brings forward the mechanical influences of the environment on the cell 
like friction, fluid dynamics, traffic etc. In our model, a cell was placed adjacent to itself post differentia-
tion and was bound to stay adjacent to it. This is not always true in biological microenvironments where 
the cells are motile in nature and exhibit movements that are directed along temperature, magnetic, 
rigidity gradients, and many more [2].

In our future modeling studies, we plan to extend our model by incorporating cellular movement 

and cellular polarity. More generally, the use of connectionist models in modeling pattern regulation 
is an important area for future research. As pointed out more than 20 years ago [11], there are many 
interesting aspects of such models that could help understand the complex cellular computations that 
underlie pattern regulations. The possibility that such models could unify the information process-
ing-level and molecular mechanism level views of morphogenesis [7,24] is exciting, and would have 
many applications in regenerative medicine, developmental biology, and synthetic bioengineering.

1.7.  Neural network
The neural skeleton forms a recurrent neural network mimicking Hopfield neural network [14]. The weights are such that 
the neural skeleton maintains a constant slope of the morphogen as it grows. A different perspective towards this concept 
would be that the states of the neurons shall be ordered in either descending or ascending order having a constant slope. In 
the simulation, this slope (λ

c1 and λc2) was used a parameter. This parameter can be thought of a genetic code encoding the 

value of the slope of the electrical voltage gradient in a neural skeleton. A key assumption we have made in our simulation 
is that this slope value is conserved over the generations and does not change with the evolution.

The idea behind this design is that it will create a staircase like structure of the morphogen gradient. In order to obtain 

a smooth gradient, the cells at the edges of the band were allowed to receive connections from both the adjacent neurons. 
The algorithm for the neural skeleton development has been summarized below.

(I)   grow the tissue and measure its length along the east–west and north–south direction.
(I )   If the measured length exceeds the previous length by a threshold value say λ, then expand the neural skeleton 

by adding one neuron. Using this slope value, calculate the weight matrix of the new neural skeleton such that each 
neuron connects only to the adjacent neuron. Now connect only those cells to neurons which are spatially close to 
that neuron. Start again from step 1. The same steps are followed when the tissue length decreases.

(III)   If the measured length does not exceed the previous length by λ, then go to step I.
